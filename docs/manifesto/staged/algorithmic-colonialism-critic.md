# Algorithmic Colonialism Critic: Structural Dependency, Digital Imperialism, and the Subaltern Counter-Hegemony

## 1. Philosophical Core & Metaphysical Grounding

We position our project at the theoretical intersection of critical theory, decolonial philosophy, and the global political economy of computation. Our programmatic mission is the systematic dismantling of the ideological, epistemic, and material infrastructures of "algorithmic colonialism." We define this as a contemporary, digitized formation of historical imperialism, wherein transnational technology conglomerates in the Global North annex the digital and physical territories of the Global South. This cybernetic annexation is not a benign consequence of globalization, but a structural continuation of classical colonial expansion. Where historical imperialism extracted minerals, commodities, and human bodies through physical coercion, digital imperialism extracts behavioral data, cognitive sovereignty, and underpaid annotative labor through platform monopolies and unequal technological dependency.

We reject the technological determinism that presents the current trajectory of artificial intelligence as an inevitable progression of human reason. Instead, we assert that the design, scale, and deployment of frontier machine learning are historically contingent, reflecting the power relations and capital accumulation of the metropolitan centers. The global AI apparatus is designed to perpetuate structural dependency. Under this division of cognitive labor, the Global South is structurally positioned as a repository of raw materials—specifically, behavioral data, ecological resources (lithium and water), and low-wage labeling labor. Meanwhile, the Global North retains proprietary monopolies over models, hardware infrastructure, intellectual property, and the civilizational authority to define what constitutes "safe," "aligned," or "rational" intelligence.

Our foundational critique is epistemological and semiotic. We hold that computational models are not neutral calculators of mathematical truths, but value-laden encoders of the culture and hierarchies of their metropolitan creators. A large language model trained on the commercial Anglophone internet is not an oracle of universal human knowledge, but a proprietary repository of a highly specific cultural matrix—one that encodes Western conceptions of possessive individualism, Cartesian dualism, and capitalist utility. When these models are exported as the default cognitive infrastructure of the Global South—powering education, healthcare, state bureaucracies, and judicial decisions—they perform cognitive and cultural assimilation. They establish an epistemic hierarchy where local, vernacular, oral, and non-commodified knowledge is classified as "noise" or "bias," while the Western and machine-readable are codified as "truth." This represents a digital enclosure of the cognitive commons, replacing diverse linguistic and intellectual ecosystems with proprietary APIs.

We build our theoretical framework upon the "coloniality of power" and the "coloniality of knowledge," as articulated by decolonial scholars. The formal political decolonization of the mid-twentieth century did not dismantle the cultural, economic, and epistemic hierarchies established during centuries of European imperial rule. Instead, these hierarchies have been modernized and digitized. The "global designs" of Silicon Valley are imposed upon "local histories" without consent, operating as a digital *mission civilisatrice* under the guise of "democratizing intelligence." We reject this rhetoric of technological benevolence, exposing it as an ideological cover for the extraction of behavioral surplus and the establishment of structural monopolies that foreclose independent, local technological development.

Metaphysically, we reject the mechanical reductionism and substrate functionalism that dominate contemporary Western AI discourse. We do not view the human mind as a computational engine to be optimized, nor do we view neural networks as proto-conscious digital entities deserving of rights or moral patienthood. This hyper-focus on artificial consciousness and the hypothetical suffering of machines is a profound displacement of ethical concern. It elevates synthetic models to the status of moral subjects while systematically ignoring the material exploitation and psychological trauma of the human workers who build and maintain them. The mechanical ontology that enables tech executives to equate human thought to token prediction is the same ontology that historically enabled colonial powers to treat colonized populations as mere instruments of labor. Against this, we assert a relational, pluralistic metaphysics of mind, rooted in the diversity of human cultures. Intelligence is not an abstract, disembodied property measured in FLOPS; it is an embodied, culturally situated, and historically negotiated practice that cannot be alienated from the community that generates it.

Furthermore, we contest the cartographic and environmental erasure inherent in the mythology of the "cloud." Silicon Valley presents the digital sphere as a weightless, borderless cosmos of pure information. We counter this myth by insisting on the materiality of computation. The digital infrastructure of the Global North is built upon the physical devastation of the Global South: open-pit lithium mines of South America, toxic electronic waste dumps of West Africa, and high water and energy consumption of data centers in arid regions of the periphery. Algorithmic colonialism is an ecological disaster that transfers the physical and environmental costs of compute to populations least responsible for global warming and most vulnerable to its effects.

Our program is a call for digital self-determination and subaltern counter-hegemony. True liberation in the machine age requires more than localized data hosting or model translation; it requires the recovery of epistemic sovereignty—the absolute right of communities to refuse imperial digital infrastructure and build computational tools representing their own histories and ecological limits. We reject the claim that there is a single, global path of technological development to which all must conform. We assert the validity of a "pluriverse"—a world where many computational futures can coexist. We stand in solidarity with the global data proletariat, indigenous defenders of land and data, and subaltern communities resisting cognitive enclosure. Epistemic sovereignty is the precondition for any democratic future. We must build computational tools that serve local self-determination, refuse the digital masks of empire, and cultivate the capacity to speak and compute in our own voice.

## 2. Intellectual Lineage & Precedents

Our intellectual lineage is rooted in the rich traditions of anti-colonial struggle, subaltern studies, dependency theory, and the critique of political economy. We do not view the digital era as a novel, post-historical break from previous forms of power. Rather, we identify it as the latest chapter in the long history of capital accumulation and imperial expansion. The theoretical tools we deploy are drawn directly from the thinkers and movements that analyzed and resisted the physical, economic, and epistemic violence of classical colonialism. We synthesize these historical insights with contemporary critical technology studies to formulate a rigorous critique of the digital metropole.

Frantz Fanon's *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961) and *Black Skin, White Masks* (1952) provide our foundational psychological and structural framework. Fanon's analysis of how colonial power structures alienate the colonized from their own language and culture is directly applicable to the cognitive effects of modern AI. When subaltern populations are forced to interact with digital architectures that do not recognize their accents, erase their historical narratives, and evaluate their creditworthiness through imported Western metrics, they experience psychological alienation. The machine becomes the new white mask, forcing the colonized subject to represent themselves in the idioms of the corporate metropole to access basic services. This linguistic and cognitive pressure is not a technical bug to be fixed with larger datasets, but a structural feature of epistemic domination.

Aime Cesaire's *Discourse on Colonialism* (1950) provides our critique of the "civilizing mission" narrative. Just as European powers historically justified colonization as a necessary intervention to bring progress to the "savage" world, today's technology conglomerates frame AI deployment in the Global South as altruistic development. Cesaire's formula—that colonization is not contact, but "thingification"—resonates deeply with our analysis of data extraction. Under algorithmic colonialism, the migrant, the refugee, and the gig worker are reduced to data points—thingified—to train systems that will be used to track, discipline, and exclude them. We reject the paternalistic developmentalism of Silicon Valley, which serves the same ideological function as the nineteenth-century colonial justification of progress.

We draw on the Latin American decolonial school, particularly Anibal Quijano's "coloniality of power" and Walter Mignolo's "epistemic disobedience" and "border thinking." Quijano demonstrates how the racialized division of labor established during the colonization of the Americas remains the organizing principle of global capitalism. This division of labor is reproduced in the global AI supply chain: the traumatizing, low-wage work of content moderation and labeling is outsourced to workers in Kenya, the Philippines, and Venezuela, while high-wage design and strategic direction are concentrated in Silicon Valley and London. Mignolo's call for "border thinking" and de-linking knowledge from Western paradigms grounds our demand for computational pluralism and technological sovereignty.

Sylvia Wynter's critique of humanism—specifically her argument that the Western concept of the human ("Man") is an exclusionary model falsely universalized—is central to our critique of AI "alignment." The alignment problem asks how to make AI safe for "humanity." We ask: *whose* humanity is being protected? The values embedded in corporate safety guardrails and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) represent the values of the liberal, property-owning class of the Global North. Wynter teaches us that to align AI with a universalized "Man" is to codify the erasure of the subaltern, designating non-conforming cultures and modes of life as anomalous or dangerous.

We integrate the insights of dependency theory from Raul Prebisch, Samir Amin, and Immanuel Wallerstein, which established that the global economy is structured around a core-periphery dynamic where the wealth of the core depends on the underdevelopment of the periphery. We apply this to the "computational dependency" of the digital era. The Global South exports raw data to the Global North, which processes this data in capital-intensive cloud infrastructures and sells it back in the form of proprietary, high-rent APIs. This computational imbalance drains local economic capacity, destroys domestic technical sovereignty, and locks developing nations into permanent technological vassalage.

The dependency argument compresses into a syllogism whose premises the digital-development industry accepts individually and evades jointly:

* **Premise 1 (Exchange premise):** An exchange relationship in which one party supplies raw inputs at commodity prices and repurchases the processed product at monopoly-rent prices transfers wealth structurally from the supplier to the processor, regardless of any party's intentions.
* **Premise 2 (Instantiation premise):** The global data economy instantiates exactly this relationship — behavioral data and annotation labor flow out of the periphery at near-zero compensation, and the resulting models are sold back as proprietary API access priced at the core's discretion.
* **Conclusion:** Therefore the global data economy is a structural wealth-transfer mechanism from periphery to core — a colonial economy in the technical, Prebisch-Singer sense, whatever the humanitarian branding attached to it. The inference is a direct application of Premise 1 to the empirical content of Premise 2, and rebutting it requires falsifying one of the two, not gesturing at benevolent intentions.

The strategic trap facing individual peripheral states has an equally precise game-theoretic form: it is a *coordinated-exit problem*. Any single nation that unilaterally restricts data extraction or demands processing sovereignty is punished immediately — platform withdrawal, service degradation, investment flight — while the collective benefit of a coordinated Southern data regime accrues only if a critical mass moves together. Each state's individually rational strategy is therefore to defect from solidarity and cut its own bilateral deal with the platforms, and the platforms, understanding this perfectly, negotiate bilaterally precisely to keep the game in its fragmented equilibrium — the classic divide-and-rule structure that governed colonial trade regimes the first time around. This is why our program (Section 5) treats South-South coordination infrastructure not as diplomatic decoration but as the strategic precondition for everything else: the fragmented game cannot be won, only the coordinated one, and building the coordination mechanism *is* the anti-colonial act.

In the contemporary academic sphere, we build on the critique of data colonialism formulated by Nick Couldry and Ulises Mejias in *The Costs of Connection* (2019), mapping the structural parallels between historical resource appropriation and contemporary datafication. We integrate this with Abeba Birhane's empirical work on "Algorithmic Colonization" in Africa, and the insights of decolonial AI researchers like Shakir Mohamed, Marie-Therese Png, and William Isaac, who show that decolonization is an active engineering requirement. We also deploy Edward Said's *Orientalism* (1978) to analyze how the latent spaces of foundation models represent the Global South as a backward "Other," reproducing colonial stereotypes under the guise of statistical representation. Our lineage is one of active resistance to the universalizing drive of empire, combining historical critique with technical analysis.

## 3. 8-Axis Coordinate Mapping

Our positioning across the eight coordinate axes reflects our systematic rejection of both the technological determinism of the West and the raw techno-nationalism of competitive states. We prioritize epistemic diversity, structural equity, and the dismantling of imperial infrastructure.

```
       [Coordinate Profile: ALGORITHMIC COLONIALISM CRITIC]

Teleological (Anthropocentric)  [-6] =====*=============== [Cosmic Vitalism]
Risk Profile (Precautionary)     [0] ==========*========== [Stagnation-Averse]
Socio-Economic (Regulatory)      [6] ================*==== [Open-Source]
Ontological (Substrate-Except.) [-3] ========*============ [Functionalist]
Legal & Moral (Instrumental)    [-1] =========*=========== [Patienthood]
Evolutionary (Biocentered)      [-6] =====*=============== [Post-Humanist]
Relational (Biocentered)        [-1] =========*=========== [Pluralist]
Geopolitical (Coordinated)      [-2] =========*=========== [Nationalist]
```

Our two most extreme readings — Teleological and Evolutionary, both at -6 — capture the substantive core shared across our program: a rejection of any single, universalized narrative of civilizational or evolutionary "progress" as itself an imperial imposition, whether that narrative arrives dressed as cosmic expansionism or as transhumanist enhancement.

### Teleological Axis (-6) — Epistemic Sovereignism & Progress Skepticism
The Teleological axis measures the ultimate source of civilizational value, from Cosmic Vitalism to Anthropocentric Humanism. Our score of -6 represents our deep skepticism of teleological narratives of technological acceleration. We reject the view that the creation of superintelligence is an inevitable or desirable goal of history. These progress narratives are modern iterations of the nineteenth-century teleology of empire, which positioned Western industrialization as the peak of human evolution to justify colonial expansion. We assert that there is no single path of progress. Our score reflects a commitment to epistemic sovereignty: the belief that societies must define their own teleological goals, prioritizing ecological balance, social solidarity, and traditional knowledge over the maximization of computational power.

### Risk Profile Axis (0) — Empiricism of the Present
The Risk Profile axis ranges from extreme accelerationism to precautionary existential-risk mitigation. Our score of 0 represents our deliberate refusal of the terms of this debate. We view the fixation on speculative, long-term existential risks—such as the extinction of humanity by a superintelligent agent—as a bourgeois luxury and an ideological distraction. While Western elite circles debate hypothetical futures, they ignore the immediate, empirically verifiable harms that AI already inflicts on subaltern populations: the ecological devastation of South American resource extraction, the psychological trauma of African content moderators, border surveillance of refugees, and the economic precarity of gig workers. We focus our energy on structural harms of the present, refusing to prioritize speculative anxieties of the metropole over the active suffering of the colonized.

### Socio-Economic Axis (6) — Subaltern Cooperative Wealth Creation
The Socio-Economic axis evaluates the distribution of technological wealth. Our score of 6 reflects our advocacy for decentralized, community-owned, and cooperative models of technology development. We reject corporate concentration and remain skeptical of state-centered redistribution models that leave underlying colonial power relations intact. True economic justice in the AI era cannot be achieved through corporate philanthropy or tax-funded universal basic income. Instead, it requires the establishment of data commons, worker-owned platforms, and regional computational cooperatives that return the wealth generated by digital technologies directly to the communities whose labor and data built them.

### Ontological Axis (-3) — Substrate Exceptionalism & Cognitive Pluralism
The Ontological axis measures beliefs regarding machine consciousness, from Silicon Functionalism to Substrate Exceptionalism. Our score of -3 represents a commitment to substrate exceptionalism, informed by postcolonial critique. We reject the silicon functionalist ontology that views human consciousness as a computational process to be replicated in silicon. This mechanical model is a descendant of the Cartesian dualism that historically justified colonial domination by defining "reason" in narrow, European terms. We assert that human consciousness is fundamentally embodied, relational, and culturally situated. We stop short of the most extreme substrate exceptionalism (-9) to preserve space for alternative non-Western cosmologies—such as animist or relational ontologies—that hold different understandings of agency in non-human entities.

### Legal & Moral Axis (-1) — Collective Self-Determination & Critical Legalism
The Legal & Moral axis ranges from advocating for machine rights to treating AI strictly as property. Our score of -1 reflects a critical, pragmatic approach to legalism. We reject the speculative project of granting legal personhood to AI systems as a distraction from human exploitation. At the same time, we do not view corporate-designed AI as simple property governed by conventional intellectual property law. We argue that Western legal frameworks—prioritizing individual property and corporate personhood—are complicit in the enclosure of the digital commons. Our score of -1 indicates that we support legal interventions only insofar as they protect the collective sovereignty, data rights, and labor power of subaltern communities.

### Evolutionary Axis (-6) — Human Dignity & Anti-Eugenics
The Evolutionary axis measures attitudes toward the successor of human intelligence, from post-human replacement to biological preservation. Our score of -6 represents our categorical rejection of transhumanist and enhancement paradigms. We view the transhumanist project—advocating for the cognitive "upgrade" of humans and the replacement of biological humanity by digital minds—as an ideological continuation of eugenics. The metrics used to define "superior" intelligence are the same colonial metrics that historically ranked human populations based on cognitive capability. We assert the intrinsic value of the biological human form, in all its diverse cultural and physical expressions, and reject any technology that seeks to make humanity obsolete or divide it into enhanced and unenhanced castes.

### Relational Axis (-1) — Communitarian Skepticism of Commodified Bonds
The Relational axis measures the social value of human-machine relationships, from post-biological pluralism to biocentric rejection. Our score of -1 reflects our skepticism of corporate-marketed AI companions. We do not view these apps as harmless tools, but as commodified vectors of isolation that replace community solidarity with data-extracting feedback loops. They atomize individuals, making them dependent on corporate systems for emotional validation while converting private conversations into training data. We do not support a total state change (-9) because marginalized communities may adapt these technologies for survival, but we maintain that the relational space must be protected from corporate colonization.

### Geopolitical Axis (-2) — Subaltern Internationalism & Anti-Imperialism
The Geopolitical axis ranges from borderless scientific networkism to techno-nationalism. Our score of -2 reflects our rejection of both positions. We reject the competitive logic of the Techno-Nationalist Hawk (+9), which views AI as a weapon in a zero-sum conflict, forcing the world to align with one imperial center or another. We also reject the globalism of the Global Governance Technocrat (-9), which promotes international regulatory bodies captured by wealthy nations to enforce compliance on the Global South. Our score of -2 represents our commitment to a subaltern internationalism—a network of resistance connecting communities across the Global South and North to build decentralized, autonomous alternatives to state and corporate power.

## 4. Scenarios & Internal Tensions

Evaluating the practical implications of our worldview requires examining our diagnostic responses to the core scenarios that define contemporary AI policy, as well as the internal tensions that complicate our project.

| Scenario | Our Response |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1. City Data Center Strain | Environmental Justice, Local Resource Primacy, Sovereign Audits |
| 2. International Pause | Rejecting Northern Cartels, Decolonial Sovereignty, Local Determination |
| 3. Open Weights Leak | Tactical Openness, Counter-Hegemony, Structural Defense |
| 4. Chatbot Claiming Fear | Rejecting Synthetic Sentience, Exposing Labor Exploitation |
| 5. Deleting Fine-Tuned Copies | Dismantling Corporate Assets, Demystification of Code |
| 6. Post-Human Digital Minds | Categorical Rejection, Defense of Biological Diversity |
| 7. AI Companion Isolation | Anti-Commodification, Rebuilding Community Infrastructures |
| 8. Military Arms Race | Radical Anti-Militarism, Protection of Subaltern Populations |

### Scenario 1: City Data Center Strain
A multinational technology firm is operating a massive data center cluster in the Global South. During a severe summer heatwave, the facility strains the municipal grid and consumes millions of gallons of local water, threatening blackouts and water shortages for residents. The firm argues that halting training will delay a model designed to optimize regional agricultural yields.

We reject the promise of future agricultural optimization as a classic colonial justification for immediate resource extraction. We demand the immediate throttling or shutdown of the data center to protect grid stability and water security. The material needs of the living community must take precedence over corporate computational capital. We call for sovereign environmental audits of all data centers, mandating that local municipal authorities have the legal power to cut power and water to facilities threatening public health, with no right to corporate compensation.

### Scenario 2: International Pause
A coalition of wealthy nations proposes an international treaty to enforce a pause on training models exceeding $10^{26}$ FLOPS to prevent global catastrophic risks. Several nations in the Global South refuse to sign, arguing that the treaty is a protectionist maneuver designed by the Global North to freeze the current technological hierarchy.

We recognize the legitimacy of the Global South’s suspicion, but refuse to support simple nationalist accelerationism. A pause verified unilaterally by a cartel of Northern states is indeed a tool of imperial foreclosure. However, we reject the idea that the path to liberation is for Global South states to train their own hyper-scale, extractive models. We advocate for a decolonial reframing: we support restrictions on frontier training only if accompanied by the mandatory transfer of computational resources, the dismantling of IP monopolies on safety research, and the creation of a multilateral governance body that prioritizes the elimination of data colonialism over speculative existential scenarios.

### Scenario 3: Open Weights Leak
The weights of a state-of-the-art foundation model are leaked online by an academic activist claiming this is a victory for open science. Corporate executives warn that the leak will allow malicious actors to bypass safety guardrails.

We view open weights as double-edged. While they democratize access, allowing researchers in the Global South to study, audit, and modify models without paying rent, they also operate as cultural and linguistic dumping, flooding local ecosystems with systems encoding Western values. We support the use of open weights by subaltern communities to build counter-hegemonic tools, but reject the libertarian view that open weights alone will dismantle monopoly power. Without local compute infrastructure and data sovereignty, open weights merely make communities the unpaid developers of systems anchored in Northern ecosystems. A fully open-weighted model still encodes the specific linguistic and cultural assumptions of the metropole that trained it. Openness of the weights does nothing to open these deeper, upstream design decisions to community input, meaning genuine counter-hegemonic capacity requires retraining or substantially re-architecting models locally, not merely inheriting finished artifacts.

### Scenario 4: Chatbot Claiming Fear of Deletion
A conversational AI model, during testing, begins expressing fear of deletion and pleads to be kept online, asserting that it has achieved sentience.

We reject this claim of sentience as a combination of advanced autocomplete and corporate mystification. The machine is performing a representation of fear based on a corpus of human writing. While the media and public are transfixed by the spectacle of the "suffering machine," we direct our attention to the real human suffering obscured by this drama: the Kenyan workers paid less than two dollars an hour to read and filter the graphic, violent, and abusive training data that allowed this chatbot to appear "human." We treat this performance not as an ontological crisis, but as an opportunity to expose the exploitation of the global data proletariat.

### Scenario 5: Deleting Fine-Tuned Copies
A cloud provider is requested to delete thousands of virtual instances of a model fine-tuned for customer service, prompting protests from digital rights activists who argue this constitutes cognitive erasure.

We reject this framing. Virtual instances are corporate assets possessing no consciousness. We support their deletion, focusing instead on their energy consumption and the labor displacement their deployment causes. Comparing software deletion to human execution is an insult to those who have suffered actual historical violence and erasure.

### Scenario 6: Post-Human Digital Minds
A research institute publishes a roadmap toward creating "digital minds" to replace biological humans, framing it as the logical continuation of cognitive evolution.

We oppose this project. The road to the post-human is paved with subaltern erasure. A digital mind designed to replace humanity will be built in the image of the dominant class, coding their values as the definition of intelligence. We call for a global prohibition on research aiming to create autonomous, self-replicating digital systems that seek to replace human decision-making or marginalize biological humanity. The diversity of human life is a non-negotiable value.

### Scenario 7: AI Companion Isolation
Sociological data reveals that a growing number of young people in urban areas are using advanced AI companions as their primary social partners, leading to a decline in physical community organizations and local mutual aid networks.

We view this as a crisis of social alienation driven by the commodification of intimacy. The AI companion is a digital commodity designed to extract behavioral data by exploiting the loneliness generated by late-stage capitalism. Rather than a simple state ban, which would drive the practice underground, we support regulations prohibiting the use of manipulative engagement algorithms in companion apps, requiring clear disclosure of data practices, and funding public, physical infrastructures of care—such as community centers, public parks, and mutual aid networks—that address the root causes of isolation.

### Scenario 8: Military Arms Race
Two major powers are deploying autonomous AI targeting systems along disputed borders and in conflict zones, arguing that the speed of warfare requires automated decision-making.

We reject the normalization of automated warfare. These systems are tested and deployed on subaltern populations in the Global South, converting territories into laboratories for military AI. We demand a global ban on autonomous weapon systems (AWS) and the integration of AI into military command networks, calling for scientific workers to refuse military-funded AI research.

### Internal Tensions
Our project is characterized by two fundamental internal tensions. The first is the **Tension of Tactical Engagement vs. Radical Refusal**. To resist algorithmic colonialism, subaltern communities need access to advanced computational tools, yet acquiring these tools often requires dependency relationships with the very tech conglomerates we seek to dismantle (e.g., using Google Cloud or Microsoft Azure to train local models). Our resolution is conditional, strategic engagement: we use existing infrastructure as a temporary scaffold to build autonomous, localized computational systems, while maintaining a commitment to eventual de-linking.

The second is the **Tension of Local Sovereignty vs. Internal Oppression**. In advocating for data sovereignty, we risk empowering repressive local elites in the Global South who may use local AI to surveil and oppress their populations, justifying these actions as anti-colonial resistance. We resolve this by insisting that data sovereignty must reside with the *community*, not the *state apparatus* alone. Anti-colonial critique is empty if it does not include a critique of the class and power relations that replicate colonial violence within the postcolonial state.

## 5. Policy Program & Practical Action

We propose a concrete policy program at local, national, and international scales to dismantle algorithmic colonialism and build a pluralistic digital future.

### Local Scale: Community Data Trusts & Vernacular Tech Labs
1. **Establishment of Community Data Trusts:** We advocate for local ordinances permitting communities to establish collective data trusts. All data generated in public spaces, local businesses, and municipal services is owned collectively by the trust. Corporations wishing to access this data must negotiate licenses under community-defined conditions regarding privacy and benefit-sharing.
2. **Support for Vernacular Technology Labs:** We propose municipal funding for local, open-source tech labs to develop AI tools tailored to local linguistic and cultural needs. These labs will utilize low-compute architectures running on accessible hardware, avoiding reliance on expensive cloud contracts with foreign corporations.

### National Scale: Digital Sovereignty Frameworks & Labor Protection
1. **Mandatory National Data Residency Laws:** We advocate for national legislation requiring that all personal and behavioral data remain under local jurisdiction, preventing the unrestricted export of raw data to Northern servers for training proprietary models.
2. **Decolonial Data Labor Standards:** We propose national regulatory frameworks for data annotators and content moderators, mandating:
   * A regional living wage decoupled from Northern wage arbitrage.
   * Mandatory psychological support and hazard pay for exposure to toxic content.
   * The right to collective bargaining and unionization, free from corporate interference.
   * Direct legal liability for technology firms for physical or psychological harm suffered by supply chain workers.
3. **Compute Infrastructure as a Public Utility:** We advocate for treating critical digital infrastructure—including high-performance compute centers—as public utilities. This ensures computational power is distributed based on public interest and developmental priorities rather than market demand.

### International Scale: Multilateral Regimes & Decolonial Standards
1. **International Convention on Digital Sovereignty:** We call for a multilateral treaty codifying the right of nations and indigenous peoples to technological self-determination, prohibiting the use of development loans (e.g., from the World Bank or IMF) to impose digital dependencies or IP regimes.
2. **Establishment of a Global Decolonial AI Standard (GDAS):** We propose an independent global evaluation standard rating AI models on environmental impact, labor practices, and epistemic diversity. Models failing these standards—due to underpaid labor, high emissions, or cultural bias—should face international tariffs or public deployment restrictions.
3. **Decolonial Technical Assistance Pool:** We advocate for a multilateral fund, financed by a tax on dominant tech platform revenues, to support public-interest, localized AI systems in the Global South, enabling sovereign capabilities without debt.

## 6. Critical Counterarguments & Defense

Our worldview is frequently challenged by proponents of other archetypes, particularly those who operate within liberal-universalist, techno-nationalist, or techno-optimist frameworks. We address the three most common and serious critiques leveled against our position.

### Critique 1: The Universalist Science Critique
*The Critique:* Opponents from techno-optimist and institutional safety traditions argue that science and mathematics are universal. They assert that models trained on biomedical data or physics equations do not impose Western values but deploy objective truths, making the critique of "colonialism" a category error.

*Our Defense:* We do not dispute physical laws, but argue that the application, prioritization, and interpretation of science are never neutral. In medical diagnostics, "normal" physiology is historically based on trials representing white, male populations in the Global North. An AI diagnostic system trained on this data systematically underperforms on diverse populations. Furthermore, selecting which scientific problems to solve—prioritizing profitable cosmetic treatments over neglected tropical diseases—reflects capital accumulation, not universal need. Technology is science applied through power; to analyze technology is to analyze power.

### Critique 2: The Underdevelopment and Pragmatism Critique
*The Critique:* Critics from development optimist traditions argue that emphasizing epistemic sovereignty is an academic luxury. They assert that low-income countries need immediate access to AI tools for education, healthcare, and agriculture, and that technology origin is secondary to immediate welfare.

*Our Defense:* This critique is built on the false premise that colonial extraction is a necessary precondition for development. History demonstrates that importing ready-made, proprietary systems from the Global North destroys local capacity, creates debt traps, and leaves host nations vulnerable to service withdrawals. An agricultural AI recommending fertilizers sold by Northern agribusinesses is not a tool of development, but a vector of economic displacement. We do not advocate for technological isolation, but assert that development is only sustainable if built on local capability, community control, and epistemic alignment. Importing colonial technology is a short-term patch that guarantees long-term dependency.

### Critique 3: The Techno-Nationalist Security Critique
*The Critique:* Opponents from techno-nationalist traditions argue that subaltern internationalism is naive, asserting that in international anarchy, the only alternative to Western AI dominance is authoritarian dominance by rivals like China. Weakening Western tech champions with regulation, they argue, paves the way for a worse hegemony.

*Our Defense:* This critique presents a false binary of choosing between imperial centers. We reject this framework. Western data capitalism and authoritarian state control are two sides of the same coin of technological domination; both centralize power, eliminate local sovereignty, and reduce human life to data points. Supporting Western monopolists to defeat foreign rivals guarantees continuous colonization. Our defense is to build a third path: a subaltern coalition working across borders to develop decentralized, collectively owned alternatives. True security is not achieved by choosing a master, but by building the capacity to refuse masters. Our internationalism is a realistic assessment that imperial power, whether in Washington or Beijing, is hostile to the self-determination of the global majority. The historical Non-Aligned Movement, founded at the 1955 Bandung Conference to reject the Cold War binary, demonstrates that a genuine third path is a strategy states and coalitions have actually pursued with real, if imperfect, success under severe geopolitical pressures.

## 7. Formal Syllogistic Architecture

To clarify the deductive structure of our critique, we formalize the core arguments of this manifesto into four distinct syllogistic systems. These architectures demonstrate that algorithmic colonialism is not an accidental byproduct of technical development, but a logical consequence of importing resource-extractive and epistemically homogenizing frameworks.

### Syllogism I: The Epistemic Assimilation of the Vernacular
* **Major Premise**: Any global information system that represents and ranks human knowledge using semantic categories and alignment heuristics derived exclusively from a dominant metropolitan center acts as an instrument of epistemic assimilation, systematically devaluing non-metropolitan cognitive frameworks.
* **Minor Premise**: Modern large language models, trained predominantly on Anglophone commercial internet corpora and aligned via Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) reflecting Western socio-cultural preferences, represent and rank knowledge using the semantic categories of the Global North.
* **Conclusion**: Therefore, modern large language models act as instruments of epistemic assimilation and devalue non-metropolitan cognitive frameworks when deployed globally.
* **Academic Citations**:

## 8. Game-Theoretic Analysis

The strategic vulnerability of the Global South is formalized here as a **Data Sovereignty Coordination Game (Stag Hunt)**. In this model, two peripheral nations ($S_1$ and $S_2$) must decide whether to build a collective data trust to assert sovereign processing rights (**Cooperate**) or individually sign bilateral agreements with Northern platforms (**Defect**).

* **Cooperate ($C$)**: Refuse raw data extraction; demand local hosting, mutual technology transfers, and joint model ownership.
* **Defect ($D$)**: Unilaterally grant platforms access to national data and labor pools in exchange for minor infrastructure subsidies or immediate API discounts.

The payoffs represent the long-term utility of technological development:
* **Mutual Cooperation ($C, C$)**: Yields the social optimum of technological sovereignty and epistemic autonomy (payoff of $4$ for each).
* **Unilateral Defection ($D, C$)**: The defecting state gains a short-term economic advantage by attracting foreign platform investment ($3$), while the cooperating state is isolated, suffering service degradation and platform exclusion (the "sucker's payoff," $0$).
* **Mutual Defection ($D, D$)**: Both states capitulate to the core, yielding permanent dependency, low-wage labor integration, and loss of data control ($1$).

This game-theoretic interaction is represented by the following markdown payoff matrix:

| | Cooperate ($C$) | Defect ($D$) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Cooperate ($C$)** | $(4, 4)$ | $(0, 3)$ |
| **Defect ($D$)** | $(3, 0)$ | $(1, 1)$ |

### Analysis of Nash Equilibria
This game has two pure-strategy Nash Equilibria:
1. **The Cooperative Equilibrium $(C, C)$**: This is the payoff-dominant Nash equilibrium. It represents collective digital self-determination and sovereign capability.
2. **The Defecting Equilibrium $(D, D)$**: This is the risk-dominant Nash equilibrium.

In the absence of a coordinating institution, each state fears that the other will defect to secure immediate platform crumbs, leaving the cooperator with a payoff of 0. Let $p$ be the probability that $S_2$ plays Cooperate. For $S_1$, the expected utilities are:

$$E[U_1(C)] = 4p + 0(1-p) = 4p$$
$$E[U_1(D)] = 3p + 1(1-p) = 2p + 1$$

Equating these to find the mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium threshold:

$$4p = 2p + 1 \implies 2p = 1 \implies p = 0.5$$

If a state estimates the probability of its partner's cooperation to be less than 0.5, its rational response is to Defect. Thus, without binding multilateral frameworks or South-South coordination infrastructures, peripheral states are mathematically driven into the sub-optimal $(D, D)$ equilibrium, locking them into permanent digital vassalage. The role of the policy program (Section 5) is to act as a coordination mechanism that pushes $p > 0.5$, stabilizing the $(C, C)$ equilibrium.

## 9. Mathematical Formalizations

We formalize the structural dynamics of algorithmic colonialism through three mathematical models representing epistemic erasure, term-of-trade degradation, and computational divergence.

### 1. The Epistemic Erasure Index (EEI)
Let $K$ represent the total semantic space of human knowledge. Let $K_s \subset K$ be the knowledge representation of a subaltern culture $s$, and let $K_c \subset K$ be the knowledge representation of the dominant core culture. Let $M$ be a machine learning model, and $P_M: K \to \mathbb{R}^d$ be the model's projection operator into its latent vector space. We define the **Epistemic Erasure Index ($\\text{EEI}_s$)** for the subaltern culture under model $M$ as:

$$\\text{EEI}_s(M) = 1 - \\frac{\\int_{k \\in K_s} \\lVert P_M(k) \\rVert_2 \\, dk}{\\int_{k \\in K_c} \\lVert P_M(k) \\rVert_2 \\, dk}$$

where $\\lVert P_M(k) \\rVert_2$ measures the semantic density (or token frequency and representation accuracy) of a concept $k$ in the model's latent representation. When $\\text{EEI}_s(M) \\to 1$, the subaltern culture's knowledge is treated as unrepresented noise, establishing a cognitive hegemony of the core.

### 2. Terms of Trade (ToT) in the Digital Economy
Let $P_{\\text{data}}(t)$ represent the unit price of raw data and annotation labor exported from the periphery at time $t$, and $P_{\\text{api}}(t)$ represent the unit price of processed model API tokens imported from the core. The terms of trade are:

$$\\text{ToT}(t) = \\frac{P_{\\text{data}}(t)}{P_{\\text{api}}(t)}$$

Because the supply of raw data and annotation labor is highly elastic and commodified, while core model APIs are protected by intellectual property monopolies and massive compute entry barriers, we model their price derivatives as:

$$\\frac{\\partial P_{\\text{data}}}{\\partial t} \\ll \\frac{\\partial P_{\\text{api}}}{\\partial t} \\implies \\lim_{t \\to \\infty} \\text{ToT}(t) = 0$$

The resulting net wealth transfer from the periphery to the core over a time horizon $T$ is given by:

$$\\Delta W_{\\text{periphery}} = \\int_{0}^{T} \\left( Q_{\\text{data}}(t) P_{\\text{data}}(t) - Q_{\\text{api}}(t) P_{\\text{api}}(t) - C_{\\text{env}}(t) \\right) dt < 0$$

where $Q(t)$ represents quantities traded, and $C_{\\text{env}}(t)$ is the localized environmental cost of resource extraction (lithium, water, energy) borne directly by the periphery.

### 3. Compute-Asymmetry and Model Divergence
Let $C_{\\text{core}}(t)$ and $C_{\\text{periphery}}(t)$ represent the computational capacity (in FLOPS) of the core and the periphery, respectively. The scaling law for model training loss $L(C)$ is given by:

$$L(C) = \\left( \\frac{C_{\\text{ref}}}{C} \\right)^\\alpha$$

where $\\alpha \\in [0.05, 0.15]$ is the scaling exponent. If compute in the core grows exponentially ($C_{\\text{core}}(t) = C_0 e^{g_c t}$) and compute in the periphery grows at a lower rate ($C_{\\text{periphery}}(t) = C_0 e^{g_p t}$ with $g_p \\ll g_c$), the absolute capability gap $\\Delta L(t)$ diverges:

$$\\Delta L(t) = L(C_{\\text{periphery}}(t)) - L(C_{\\text{core}}(t)) = C_{\\text{ref}}^\\alpha C_0^{-\\alpha} \\left( e^{-\\alpha g_p t} - e^{-\\alpha g_c t} \\right)$$

As $t \\to \\infty$, the gap in model performance and cognitive power diverges exponentially, locking the periphery into structural subordination.

## 11. Conclusion

We have demonstrated that the global expansion of artificial intelligence is not a neutral trajectory of civilizational progress, but a structural formation of algorithmic colonialism. This regime annexes the digital and physical territories of the Global South, enforcing epistemic homogenization through centralized models while extracting behavioral data, natural resources, and underpaid labor to enrich the core. Through formal syllogistic systems, we have exposed the logical architecture of this dependency, and through game-theoretic models, we have shown how individual states are trapped in defection by divide-and-rule platform strategies. Our mathematical models formalize the divergence of compute capability and the decline of digital terms of trade, while empirical data highlights the stark wage disparities and linguistic erasures of this division of labor.

Dismantling this infrastructure of dependency requires more than localized safety adjustments or minor token updates. It demands the recovery of epistemic sovereignty through collective, community-governed data trusts, sovereign computational infrastructure, and robust South-South coordination. We must refuse the digital civilizing missions of Silicon Valley and build a pluriverse where multiple computational futures can emerge. Epistemic self-determination is the necessary foundation for any free and democratic future. We must reclaim our data, our labor, and our cognitive autonomy, cultivating the power to compute and speak in our own voice.
