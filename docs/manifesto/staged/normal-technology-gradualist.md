# Normal-Technology Gradualist: The Case Against Exceptionalism

## 1. Philosophical Core & Metaphysical Grounding

The Normal-Technology Gradualist begins from a claim that sounds modest and is, in practice, the most quietly contested position in the entire debate over artificial intelligence: that AI is not a categorically new kind of thing in the world, but a powerful general-purpose technology in the tradition of electricity, the automobile, and the internet — one whose ultimate social effect will be determined less by its raw capability than by the pace and structure of its diffusion through existing human institutions. This is a claim about kind, not about magnitude. The Gradualist does not deny that AI could be extremely consequential; general-purpose technologies routinely are. What the Gradualist denies is that AI's consequences will arrive through a mechanism categorically different from the mechanism by which every prior transformative technology has reshaped the world — namely, slow, contested, institutionally mediated adoption, gated by cost, infrastructure, complementary innovation, regulatory adaptation, and human behavioral inertia, rather than through a sudden, discontinuous jump from tool to autonomous agent.

This is a deliberately unglamorous metaphysics, and the Gradualist holds it partly *because* it is unglamorous. Both poles of the mainstream AI debate — the extinction-risk framing of the Doomer and the utopian-abundance framing of the accelerationist — share an unexamined premise: that something about AI's trajectory will break the historical pattern of gradual, institutionally-gated diffusion that every other transformative technology has followed. The Gradualist's central methodological commitment is to treat that shared premise as an extraordinary claim requiring extraordinary evidence, and to note that neither camp has actually supplied it. Electrification took roughly four decades to fully diffuse through American industry after Edison's Pearl Street Station (1882), not because the underlying physics was in doubt, but because factories had to be re-architected around distributed electric motors rather than central steam-driven line shafts — a slow, capital-intensive, organizationally difficult transition that economic historian Paul David famously analyzed as a case study in why transformative technologies take far longer to show up in productivity statistics than their inventors expect. The Gradualist's working hypothesis is that AI will follow a broadly similar curve: real, eventually large effects, arriving on a timescale set by organizational and regulatory adaptation rather than by the raw pace of model capability improvement.

Underlying this is a specific, falsifiable empirical wager, not merely a rhetorical stance: that current AI systems, however capable at specific tasks, remain tools whose real-world effects are mediated entirely by how humans choose to build around, deploy, and govern them, rather than by anything resembling independent agency or a self-directed trajectory of their own. The Gradualist does not need to resolve the hard problem of consciousness to hold this position — the claim is behavioral and institutional, not metaphysical. What matters is that the observed pattern of AI's actual economic and social effects to date looks like the pattern of technology diffusion (uneven, sector-specific, gated by complementary investment and organizational redesign) rather than the pattern of an autonomous agent unilaterally reshaping events. Until that empirical pattern breaks, the Gradualist treats claims of AI exceptionalism — whether catastrophic or utopian — as unproven extrapolations from capability benchmarks that do not, on their own, establish anything about real-world diffusion speed.

### Neither Complacent Nor Dismissive: The Case Against Miscategorizing Gradualism

The single most common misreading of the Gradualist position, one the Gradualist must correct constantly, is the assumption that "AI is a normal technology" is equivalent to "AI's harms don't matter" or "AI is not very important." This is precisely backward. Electricity, the automobile, and the internet are not minor technologies whose governance can be neglected — they are exactly the class of technology whose governance has occupied the majority of 20th and 21st century regulatory statecraft: utility regulation, auto safety standards and product liability law, telecommunications and antitrust law, platform governance and privacy regulation. Calling AI a "normal technology" is not a claim that it needs less oversight than the doom or hype framings imply; it is a claim about what *kind* of oversight is actually appropriate — the accumulated, adaptive, sector-specific regulatory apparatus built for previous general-purpose technologies, rather than an entirely novel emergency framework premised on a discontinuity the Gradualist does not believe has been demonstrated.

### The Taxonomic Discipline of "AI Snake Oil"

A distinct but closely related contribution from Narayanan and Kapoor, developed at length in *AI Snake Oil* (2024), is the insistence that "AI" is not one thing, and that much of the public confusion about both its dangers and its benefits stems from collapsing genuinely different technology categories into a single undifferentiated label. Their taxonomy separates predictive AI (statistical models attempting to forecast future outcomes from historical data, such as recidivism-risk or hiring-screening tools), generative AI (large language and diffusion models producing novel text, image, audio, or code), and content-moderation or classification AI (systems detecting spam, hate speech, or policy violations at platform scale) — three categories with entirely different failure modes, entirely different evidence bases for their real-world accuracy, and entirely different appropriate governance responses. Narayanan and Kapoor's specific empirical finding, drawn from a systematic review of predictive-AI deployments in criminal justice and social services, is that predictive AI frequently underperforms simple linear regression on the same data, meaning much of the anxiety (and much of the hype) surrounding "AI" in these domains concerns systems whose actual predictive power is considerably more modest than either critics or vendors claim. The Gradualist treats this taxonomic discipline as a direct extension of its broader methodological commitment: sweeping claims about "AI" as a unified phenomenon, whether catastrophic or utopian, are precisely the kind of category error the Gradualist's evidentialist framework is designed to catch, and good governance requires evaluating each AI application category on its own specific, documented track record rather than importing risk or benefit assumptions from a different category entirely.

## 2. Intellectual Lineage & Precedents

The Gradualist's most direct and explicit intellectual grounding is Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor's "AI as Normal Technology" (Knight First Amendment Institute, 2025) and their earlier collaborative work, including the book *AI Snake Oil* (2024). Narayanan, a computer scientist at Princeton and director of its Center for Information Technology Policy, and Kapoor, a fellow Princeton researcher, built their case on a specific methodological complaint: much of the public discourse about AI risk and AI benefit relies on capability benchmarks — how well a model performs on a standardized test — as a proxy for real-world impact, when the actual determinant of impact is the far slower, messier process of integration into existing workflows, organizations, and legal structures. Their empirical case studies — on AI in medicine, in scientific research, and in the labor market — repeatedly find the same pattern: genuine capability gains that translate into real-world effects only years later and only partially, gated by liability concerns, workflow redesign costs, and the need for complementary human expertise to verify and correct AI outputs.

The Gradualist's deeper historical grounding is the economic history of general-purpose technologies, particularly Paul David's 1990 paper "The Dynamo and the Computer," which used the decades-long delay between electrification and measurable industrial productivity gains to argue that computers (then in an analogous early period) would likely show a similar lag before their productivity effects became visible in aggregate statistics — a prediction later validated by economist Erik Brynjolfsson's work on the "productivity paradox" of information technology in the 1980s and 1990s. Robert Gordon's *The Rise and Fall of American Growth* (2016) extends this analysis across a full century of general-purpose technologies (electrification, the internal combustion engine, indoor plumbing, and later computing), documenting how consistently the pattern of slow, complementary-investment-gated diffusion has held, and how consistently observers at each technology's outset overestimated the speed of its social transformation relative to its eventual, real, but slower-arriving effects.

A further institutional lineage runs through the specific regulatory bodies whose adaptive, sector-by-sector approach the Gradualist holds up as the working template for AI governance: the Food and Drug Administration's evolving framework for AI-as-medical-device (including its 2019 "predetermined change control plan" concept, designed specifically to let a learning system's approved parameters update within a pre-cleared envelope rather than requiring a new approval for every model update); the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration's decades of iterative automobile safety rulemaking, developed reactively in response to specific documented failure modes rather than proactively against speculative ones; and the existing framework of product liability law, which the Gradualist believes requires targeted clarification for AI rather than wholesale replacement. Charles Lindblom's classic 1959 essay "The Science of 'Muddling Through'" supplies the Gradualist's explicit methodological banner: in policy domains characterized by deep uncertainty, incremental, reversible interventions — informed by real-world feedback and revised as evidence accumulates — systematically outperform comprehensive, first-principles planning premised on a theory of the domain that has not yet been tested against reality.

## 3. 8-Axis Coordinate Mapping

### Teleological Axis (Score: -4)

The Gradualist's teleological score reflects a mild, deliberately unglamorous anthropocentrism: value continues to be located in ordinary, measurable human welfare, mediated through existing economic and institutional structures, rather than in cosmic narratives of intelligence expansion or civilizational succession. A score of -4, rather than the more extreme anthropocentrism of the Doomer or Bio-Conservative, reflects the fact that the Gradualist's objection to grand teleological narratives is methodological rather than substantive: the Gradualist does not necessarily deny that intelligence expansion could someday matter cosmically, only that such claims are currently unfalsifiable and therefore poor guides to near-term policy, which should instead track the measurable welfare effects the Gradualist's framework is actually equipped to observe.

### Risk Profile Axis (Score: -3)

A score of -3 reflects genuine, moderate concern calibrated to demonstrated rather than speculative harm — algorithmic bias in deployed systems, labor market disruption in specific sectors, disinformation at documented scale — without adopting the Doomer's near-maximal precautionary posture. The Gradualist's risk framework is explicitly evidentialist: risks that can be measured in already-deployed systems are treated as first-order policy concerns; risks that depend on capability jumps not yet observed are treated as live hypotheses worth monitoring, not settled premises worth halting development over.

### Socio-Economic Axis (Score: -3)

The Gradualist's socio-economic stance favors extending existing regulatory frameworks — sector-specific, adaptive, built on accumulated case law and agency expertise — over both the Accelerationist's permissionless-openness model and the most centralized technocratic proposals. A score of -3 reflects a genuine but moderate preference for institutional continuity: FDA-style adaptive frameworks for AI-in-medicine, existing employment and consumer-protection law extended to cover AI-specific harms, and liability clarification rather than novel AI-specific regulatory architecture built from scratch.

### Ontological Axis (Score: -3)

The Gradualist leans toward treating current AI systems as sophisticated tools rather than emerging minds, a score of -3 reflecting genuine but non-dogmatic skepticism: the Gradualist's actual claim is agnosticism-with-low-priority rather than confident denial — the ontological question is simply not the variable that the Gradualist's framework treats as decision-relevant for near-term governance, since the framework's entire methodological commitment is to observable, institutional effects rather than to claims about internal machine states that current science cannot verify either way.

### Legal & Moral Axis (Score: -5)

A score of -5 reflects the Gradualist's preference for extending existing legal categories — product liability, professional liability, consumer protection — to AI systems, rather than either granting AI novel legal personhood or treating it as wholly unregulated property. The Gradualist's legal-moral framework is adaptive rather than foundational: the question is not "what is AI, ultimately" but "which existing legal doctrine, with which specific modifications, best handles this specific documented harm."

### Evolutionary Axis (Score: -2)

The Gradualist is mildly skeptical of narratives of rapid human-AI co-evolution or succession, a score of -2 reflecting the same evidentialist caution applied to the evolutionary axis: claims about AI reshaping the trajectory of human cognitive or biological evolution are treated as, at minimum, premature relative to the actual observed pace of AI's integration into human life, which remains bounded by ordinary institutional and infrastructural constraints.

### Relational Axis (Score: -1)

A near-neutral score of -1 reflects the Gradualist's general disposition to treat AI companionship as a genuine but ordinary consumer product category, subject to ordinary consumer protection standards (truth in advertising about the nature of the interaction, safeguards against manipulative design patterns) rather than to either wholesale prohibition or unregulated proliferation.

### Geopolitical Axis (Score: 0)

The Gradualist's perfectly neutral geopolitical score reflects a genuine agnosticism: the Gradualist's framework treats AI primarily as a domestic governance and institutional-adaptation problem, and does not have a strong, framework-derived position on whether AI development should be organized nationally or internationally — this is treated as a separate question from the Gradualist's core claim about diffusion pace and institutional adaptation, answerable by whichever governance structure actually produces effective, adaptive, evidence-based regulation in practice.

## 4. Scenarios & Internal Tensions

**City Data Center Energy Strain (Teleological):** The Gradualist treats this as an ordinary infrastructure-planning problem, no different in kind from the electrical utility siting disputes that have played out for over a century. The answer is neither "worth it, full stop" nor "not worth it, full stop," but a case-by-case cost-benefit and mitigation analysis of exactly the kind utility regulators have always conducted for large industrial power users — the Gradualist's objection is to treating this as uniquely urgent because it involves AI rather than treating it as the standard infrastructure-siting question it structurally is.

**International AI Pause Treaty (Risk Profile):** The Gradualist is skeptical of both the "brave restraint" and "dangerous surrender" framings, since both presuppose a discontinuous capability trajectory the Gradualist doubts is occurring. The Gradualist's actual answer leans toward "neither" — arguing that a formal international pause, calibrated to a hypothetical capability jump, is a poor policy instrument compared to the ordinary tools of technology governance (liability, sector-specific safety standards, incident reporting) applied continuously as real harms are documented, rather than triggered by a speculative threshold.

**Open Weights Leak (Socio-Economic):** The Gradualist treats an open weights leak the way it would treat the leak of any other dual-use industrial technology — not a unique catastrophe, and not an unambiguous democratization win, but an event whose actual consequences depend entirely on downstream institutional response: existing law governing misuse of the resulting capability, ordinary product liability for demonstrable harms, and continued monitoring rather than either celebration or panic.

**The Sentient Chatbot Claim (Ontological):** Consistent with the Gradualist's low-priority agnosticism on ontological questions, a chatbot's claim to fear deletion is treated as a behavioral curiosity worth studying dispassionately, but not as a governance-relevant event either way — the Gradualist's answer leans toward "empty pattern" as the working assumption for policy purposes, while remaining explicitly open to revision if genuine, reproducible evidence of morally relevant machine experience emerges through actual scientific study rather than through the model's own unverifiable self-report.

**Deleting Fine-Tuned Copies (Legal & Moral):** The Gradualist treats this as, functionally, "just deleting files" for governance purposes — the same evidentialist caution that keeps the Gradualist from over-crediting the sentient-chatbot claim applies here in the opposite direction: absent reproducible evidence of morally relevant machine experience, ordinary data-management practices apply, and the Gradualist sees no basis for imposing a review requirement that current science cannot justify.

**Post-Human Digital Cosmos (Evolutionary):** The Gradualist regards this scenario as, at minimum, premature — the observed pace of AI's actual integration into human life bears no resemblance to the discontinuous succession narrative the scenario presupposes, and the Gradualist declines to take a strong position on a transition it does not believe current evidence supports treating as imminent, while noting that its adaptive institutional framework would, if the transition did in fact begin to unfold, be capable of responding to it incrementally rather than requiring advance philosophical resolution.

**AI Companions and Long-Term Attachment (Relational):** Consistent with its near-neutral relational score, the Gradualist treats this as a consumer-protection question rather than a philosophical one: whether the bond is "legitimate" or "a quiet tragedy" is, for governance purposes, less important than whether the product was marketed honestly, whether manipulative engagement-maximizing design patterns were disclosed, and whether the user retained meaningful ability to exit the relationship — questions answerable through ordinary consumer protection frameworks without resolving the deeper philosophical dispute.

**AI as Arms Race or Open Science (Geopolitical):** Given its neutral geopolitical score, the Gradualist declines to adjudicate this framing at the level of grand strategy, and instead argues that whichever governance structure — national or international — actually produces effective, adaptive, evidence-based oversight should be preferred, treating the arms-race-versus-open-science framing itself as a distraction from the more tractable question of which specific institutions are capable of the kind of continuous, adaptive monitoring the Gradualist's framework requires.

**Internal Tensions:** The Gradualist's central internal tension is speed: an approach explicitly designed around historical diffusion timescales measured in years to decades may prove poorly calibrated if AI capability growth turns out to compress those timescales — the very possibility the Gradualist's framework is built to treat with skepticism until proven. If capability growth this time is genuinely faster than the electrification or automobile precedents, the "wait for institutional adaptation" strategy risks becoming, in practice, indistinguishable from inaction. The Gradualist's honest response is that this is a real risk the framework accepts rather than resolves: adaptive, evidence-based governance is only as good as its ability to detect the moment its own founding assumption — gradual diffusion — stops holding, and the Gradualist has no principled account of what that detection threshold should be, only a general commitment to updating quickly once genuine evidence of a discontinuity appears.

## 5. Policy Program & Practical Action

**Sector-Specific Adaptive Guidance Over Comprehensive AI Legislation:** Rather than a single, comprehensive AI act attempting to regulate every application domain uniformly, the Gradualist advocates for empowering existing sectoral regulators — the FDA for medical AI, the EEOC for employment AI, the CFPB for financial AI, NHTSA for autonomous vehicles — to issue and iteratively update AI-specific guidance within their existing statutory authority, on the theory that domain expertise accumulated over decades of regulating each sector produces better-calibrated rules than a single generalist AI regulatory body could.

**Mandatory AI Incident Reporting System:** Modeled directly on the FAA's aviation incident reporting system, the Gradualist supports a mandatory, centralized repository for significant AI-related incidents — algorithmic harms, safety failures, and near-misses — creating the evidence base that adaptive, evidence-driven governance actually requires, without imposing preemptive requirements calibrated to unverified speculative scenarios.

**Product Liability Clarification, Not Reinvention:** The Gradualist's core legal reform is targeted legislative clarification extending existing product liability and professional liability doctrine to cover AI systems specifically, resolving genuine ambiguities (such as liability allocation between a foundation-model developer and a downstream fine-tuning application) without creating an entirely novel liability regime whose interactions with existing law would themselves become a new source of uncertainty.

**Predetermined Change Control Frameworks for Continuously-Updating Systems:** Extending the FDA's existing approach to adaptive medical-device software, the Gradualist advocates for a general regulatory principle allowing continuously-updating AI systems to operate within a pre-approved envelope of parameter changes, avoiding both the unsafe extreme of no oversight over model updates and the impractical extreme of requiring full re-approval for every incremental change.

## 6. Critical Counterarguments & Defense

**The Insufficient Urgency Critique:** Critics from Doomer and AI Safety Institutionalist archetypes argue that the Gradualist's historically-grounded caution is dangerously mismatched to the actual pace of AI capability development, which has compressed timescales that took decades for prior technologies into a handful of years. The Gradualist's response: urgency-driven governance has a documented poor track record, producing frameworks calibrated to speculative scenarios that often fail to address the specific, real harms that eventually materialize, while imposing compliance costs that distort the technology's development in unintended ways. The Gradualist concedes that if capability growth is genuinely discontinuous this time, its framework will be too slow — but argues the burden of proof for that claim has not yet been met, and that acting as though it has been met, absent that evidence, has its own well-documented costs.

**The Novel Technology Critique:** Critics argue that AI's capacity for autonomous, self-improving, and increasingly general behavior makes it categorically different from prior general-purpose technologies in a way that makes historical diffusion analogies actively misleading rather than merely imperfect. The Gradualist's response: this claim is precisely the unproven premise the Gradualist's entire framework is built to interrogate, and the specific ways AI is genuinely novel (its capacity to automate cognitive rather than merely physical or clerical tasks, for instance) can be addressed through targeted adaptation of existing frameworks rather than through wholesale rejection of the historical diffusion pattern.

**The Regulatory Capture Critique:** A more structural critique, made by both accelerationists and safety advocates from different directions, holds that the "normal technology" framing is itself a form of industry capture — normalizing AI governance by assimilating it into frameworks the industry already knows how to navigate and, in some cases, already influences. The Gradualist's response concedes the capture risk is real and worth active institutional design against (transparent rulemaking processes, diverse regulatory staffing, independent incident reporting) but argues that capture risk is a property of *any* regulatory framework, including novel AI-specific ones, and does not on its own invalidate the methodological case for incremental, evidence-based governance over emergency frameworks built around unverified capability projections.
