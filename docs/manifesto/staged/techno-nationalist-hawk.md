# Techno-Nationalist Hawk: Defending Sovereign AI Supremacy in a Zero-Sum World

## 1. Philosophical Core & Metaphysical Grounding

The Techno-Nationalist Hawk occupies a unique and uncomfortable position in this framework's taxonomy: it is the archetype that takes both AI capability and civilizational competition with dead seriousness, while refusing the utopian naivety of either the e/acc Maximalist or the Effective Altruist Longtermist. Its philosophical core is Hobbesian realism applied to the 21st century's defining strategic contest. The world is not a community of cooperating states that share a common interest in managed AI development; it is an anarchic system in which states compete for survival, and in which technological superiority is the master variable that determines which states survive and which are absorbed, dominated, or destroyed. This is not a failure of international relations but its persistent structural condition: there is no Leviathan above nations to enforce treaty obligations, so treaties are only as durable as the interests that sustain them.

For the Techno-Nationalist Hawk, artificial intelligence is the most consequential force multiplier in human history — comparable not merely to the printing press or the steam engine, but to gunpowder and nuclear weapons in its capacity to concentrate decisive advantage in the hands of those who develop it first and best. The state that achieves AGI or transformative AI supremacy gains not merely economic advantages but strategic omnipotence: the ability to dominate surveillance, cyber-offense, autonomous weapons systems, logistics, and information operations at scales that make conventional military competition obsolete. This is not a speculative future scenario; it is the current trajectory. China's state-directed AI development, its integration of AI into military doctrine, and its strategic semiconductor decoupling strategy demonstrate that at least one major power has internalized this lesson. The Hawk's central claim is that the United States (and its allies) cannot afford to treat AI governance as a global commons problem when its primary strategic competitor is racing toward dominance.

The Hawk's epistemology of intelligence is functionalist in the narrow military-strategic sense: it cares primarily about what AI systems *can do* — plan, deceive, surveil, adapt, automate — rather than about what they *are* ontologically. Whether an AI system is conscious, morally significant, or philosophically interesting is beside the point when that system can guide a hypersonic missile, generate targeted disinformation at civilizational scale, or break asymmetric encryption. The Hawk is neither a catastrophist nor a techno-optimist; it is a strategist, and strategists evaluate technology through the lens of power and vulnerability. The hard problem of consciousness is a luxury of peacetime philosophy.

The Hawk's vision of civilizational progress is explicitly competitive and relative: progress means maintaining or extending strategic advantage over potential adversaries. This is not malevolence but realism. In a world where advanced AI capabilities are concentrated in the hands of authoritarian states, democratic values — free expression, individual rights, due process — are existentially threatened. The Hawk argues that the defense of liberal democratic civilization requires the *willingness* to develop and deploy AI systems in ways that may be uncomfortable from a purely rights-based or precautionary framework. The choice is not between a world with aggressive AI development and a world without it; it is between a world where democratic states lead and one where authoritarian states do.

### Technology as the Substrate of Regime Type, Not Just Power

A further, more specific claim distinguishes the Hawk from a generic realist: the belief that AI is not merely a power-multiplier neutral to regime type, but a technology whose deployment patterns actively reshape the domestic character of the states that build it. The Hawk points to China's integration of facial recognition, social credit scoring, and predictive policing as evidence that authoritarian states derive a *structural* advantage from advanced AI that democratic states do not — because authoritarian regimes face none of the due-process, civil-liberties, or electoral accountability frictions that slow democratic deployment of the same technology. This asymmetry means that a purely neutral, "may the best model win" competitive framework is not actually neutral: it advantages whichever regime type can deploy AI with the fewest internal constraints, which is almost definitionally the less democratic one. The Hawk's response is not to abandon democratic constraints — the Hawk explicitly rejects the argument that the U.S. should simply out-authoritarian its rivals — but to argue that democratic states must compensate for this structural disadvantage through greater state coordination and investment than a purely laissez-faire domestic policy would produce, precisely because the alternative is not a level playing field but a tilted one that favors Beijing by default.

### The Thucydides Trap and the Logic of Structural Rivalry

The Hawk's account of *why* the current moment is so dangerous, rather than merely competitive in an ordinary sense, borrows heavily from Graham Allison's "Thucydides Trap" framework (*Destined for War*, 2017): the historical pattern, observed across sixteen of the last twenty comparable cases since 1500, in which a rising power's challenge to an established power's dominance produces war — not because either side necessarily wants conflict, but because the structural dynamics of the transition itself generate mutual fear, worst-case planning, and escalation spirals that neither side can unilaterally halt. The Hawk applies this template directly to the U.S.-China AI relationship: China's rise as a peer or near-peer AI competitor to the United States recreates exactly the structural conditions Allison identifies as historically most dangerous, and the Hawk's policy program is explicitly designed as a Thucydides Trap management strategy — not to avoid competition, which the Hawk regards as already locked in by the structure of the situation, but to ensure that the United States enters any resulting confrontation, cold or hot, from a position of technological strength rather than technological parity or inferiority, on the theory that parity is what produces the most dangerous and unstable transitions, while clear supremacy re-stabilizes the system the way American nuclear and economic dominance did after 1945.

John Mearsheimer's offensive realism supplies the Hawk's account of state motivation more generally: great powers do not seek merely to survive, in Mearsheimer's account, but to maximize their relative power, because in an anarchic system without a reliable enforcer of agreements, the only true guarantee of security is preponderance. A state that settles for parity is a state that has bet its survival on the good faith of a rival with every incentive to defect the moment defection becomes advantageous. The Hawk reads AI compute, chip fabrication capacity, and frontier model capability as the newest currency of relative power in Mearsheimer's sense, no different in kind from battleship tonnage in 1910 or missile throw-weight in 1975 — quantifiable, trackable, and directly convertible into strategic advantage.

## 2. Intellectual Lineage & Precedents

The intellectual roots of the Techno-Nationalist Hawk run deep through Western political theory, military strategy, and industrial policy. The primary philosophical ancestor is Thomas Hobbes, whose *Leviathan* (1651) established the foundational argument that security — the prevention of the "war of all against all" — is the precondition of all other values. Hobbes's sovereign exists not to maximize welfare but to prevent the dissolution of civil order. The Hawk applies this logic to the international system: in the absence of a world government, states must provide their own security, and in the AI era, providing security means ensuring that you, not your adversary, possess the decisive military-technological edge.

Alexander Hamilton's *Report on Manufactures* (1791) provides the template for state-directed industrial development. Hamilton argued that Britain's manufacturing supremacy was a strategic threat to the newly independent United States, and that targeted tariffs, subsidies, and state investment were necessary to build domestic industries capable of sustaining genuine sovereignty. The parallel to the contemporary semiconductor and AI supply chain is direct: a nation that depends on foreign supply chains for the hardware substrate of its AI systems is strategically vulnerable in the same way that a nation dependent on foreign food imports is vulnerable to blockade.

The Cold War's defense science establishment — Vannevar Bush's *Science, the Endless Frontier* (1945), the creation of DARPA (1958), the Manhattan Project's institutional lessons — provides the modern template for state-directed technological competition. DARPA invented the internet, GPS, and touchscreens not despite being a military agency but because of it: mission-driven, long-time-horizon, technically ambitious state investment can produce civilian spillovers that markets alone would never fund. The Hawk advocates for a return to this model, directed at AI and semiconductor supremacy.

Contemporary strategists who explicitly inhabit this worldview include Eric Schmidt (former Google CEO and co-author of *The Age of AI*), who co-chaired the National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence (NSCAI) and whose final report (2021) explicitly framed AI leadership as essential to U.S. national security. The NSCAI report's recommendations — massive federal investment in AI R&D, talent pipelines, semiconductor manufacturing, and military AI integration — represent the Hawk's policy bible. Similarly, the Export Administration Regulations (EAR) updates under the Biden and Trump administrations, progressively restricting advanced semiconductor exports to China, represent Hawk philosophy implemented as policy. Jake Sullivan's 2022 "small yard, high fence" doctrine — articulated in his remarks to the Special Competitive Studies Project — gave this approach its clearest official statement: rather than attempting to restrict the entire universe of dual-use technology, the state should identify a narrow set of chokepoint capabilities and defend that narrow perimeter with maximal rigor, a formulation the Hawk regards as the correct operationalization of its own strategic instincts, even where the Hawk would push for a wider fence than Sullivan's formulation implies.

## 3. 8-Axis Coordinate Mapping

### Teleological Axis (Score: +4)

The Hawk does not occupy the cosmic teleology of the e/acc Maximalist or the Longtermist, but neither is it purely conservative or backward-looking. A score of +4 reflects a *conditional* and *nationally bounded* progressivism: the Hawk believes in technological advancement as a good, but insists that this advancement must be channeled through institutions capable of ensuring that its benefits accrue to sovereign democratic states rather than to adversaries or to globally diffuse networks. The Hawk's telos is not cosmic intelligence but civilizational security and, ultimately, the preservation and extension of liberal democratic order. This is a positive vision, but one that requires strategic constraint to achieve.

### Risk Profile Axis (Score: -6)

A score of -6 reflects the Hawk's genuine but differently structured risk calculus. Unlike the Doomer (who fears internal AI misalignment) or the AI Safety Institutionalist (who fears distributed systemic risks), the Hawk's primary risk framework is geopolitical: the risk of strategic subordination to an adversarial AI superpower. The Hawk *does* take catastrophic risks seriously — biological weapons, autonomous kill chains without human oversight, AI-enabled nuclear command vulnerabilities — but frames these risks as arguments for *controlled AI development under democratic auspices*, not for the kind of multilateral pauses that would freeze the current technology landscape. A pause that freezes the U.S. in place while China continues development is not a safety measure; it is a strategic surrender.

### Socio-Economic Axis (Score: -5)

The Hawk's socio-economic stance is explicitly dirigiste: the state should actively direct AI and semiconductor development through industrial policy, rather than leaving it to market forces or open-source community norms. A score of -5 reflects this preference for managed, state-aligned development over free-market distribution. The Hawk is not hostile to capitalism per se — it values the dynamism of private sector innovation — but insists that this dynamism must be organized in ways consistent with national security. This means export controls, national champion consolidation, security clearance requirements for sensitive AI research, and robust government procurement preferences for domestically produced AI systems.

### Ontological Axis (Score: -3)

The Hawk is largely instrumentalist on questions of AI consciousness and moral status, and treats the question as a distraction from operational reality. A score of -3 reflects a system-as-tool default: AI systems are currently instruments of escalating capability, and the question of their moral status is not directly relevant to strategic decision-making at present scales. The Hawk may become more interested in the ontological question as AI systems become genuinely autonomous — because the question of whether an AI system is a moral patient has direct implications for questions of command responsibility in military deployments. But for now, the Hawk treats ontological questions as deferred academic concerns that should not be permitted to slow operational deployment.

### Legal & Moral Axis (Score: -4)

The Hawk's legal-moral stance reflects a willingness to operate in legally gray zones that other archetypes categorically reject, without embracing the most extreme instrumentalism of a pure Corporate AI Pragmatist. A score of -4 does not mean the Hawk advocates for lawlessness, but it does mean the Hawk is willing to subordinate civil liberties considerations, international law constraints, and conventional ethical frameworks to security imperatives when national survival is at stake. The Hawk supports targeted AI surveillance programs, autonomous weapons systems with human oversight that may be thin in time-critical situations, and AI-enabled information operations (including disinformation) as instruments of strategic deterrence. This is the archetype's most politically controversial stance.

### Evolutionary Axis (Score: -2)

The Hawk is broadly conservative on human enhancement and the integration of AI into human cognitive architecture. A score of -2 reflects a preference for maintaining clear chains of command and human accountability in military and governmental systems, even as AI capabilities expand. The Hawk is not categorically opposed to AI augmentation of human soldiers or intelligence analysts, but insists that such augmentation remain reversible, auditable, and subordinate to human authority structures.

### Relational Axis (Score: -3)

A score of -3 reflects genuine skepticism about AI companions and relational AI, primarily on security grounds: AI companions designed and deployed by adversarial states represent a significant intelligence-gathering and influence vector, capable of profiling users' emotional vulnerabilities and political sympathies at scale. The Hawk supports domestic AI companionship under security review, but is deeply suspicious of cross-border emotional AI relationships, and would classify any foreign-developed companion application achieving mass domestic adoption as a national security review priority regardless of its ostensible purpose.

### Geopolitical Axis (Score: +9)

The Hawk's highest score reflects the centrality of the geopolitical dimension to its entire worldview. Unlike the AI Safety Institutionalist (who uses geopolitical framing to argue for multilateral governance) or the e/acc Maximalist (who rejects geopolitical framing altogether), the Hawk places geopolitical competition at the center of every AI policy question. A score of +9 reflects not mere nationalism but a sophisticated understanding of how technology shapes the balance of power: semiconductor supply chains, AI training compute concentrations, and the geographic distribution of AI research talent are all strategic variables that should be managed as actively as nuclear arsenals.

## 4. Scenarios & Internal Tensions

**City Data Center Energy Strain (Teleological):** The Hawk views energy infrastructure for AI compute as a national security asset. Local grid constraints should be addressed through emergency federal infrastructure authority — similar to wartime industrial mobilization — rather than through market processes that may be too slow or locally captured. The Hawk's answer to "worth it or not" is unambiguously "worth it," but frames the justification in strategic rather than abstract terms: the model being trained is not merely "bigger" in some vague sense, it is a specific increment of national capability relative to a specific rival's capability, and the grid strain is the cost of remaining in the race.

**International AI Pause Treaty (Risk Profile):** The Hawk is the most vocally opposed archetype to any international pause agreement, and answers the "brave restraint or dangerous surrender" framing with an unhesitating "surrender." Its reasoning: a pause is unverifiable (adversaries will continue under cover of civilian research), asymmetrically applied (authoritarian states face no domestic political pressure to comply), and strategically catastrophic (it freezes U.S. capability development while leaving adversaries free to advance). The Hawk's alternative is not "no limits" but *domestic* capability development governed by security clearances, export controls, and military-civil integration requirements.

**Open Weights Leak (Socio-Economic):** For the Hawk, an open weights leak of a frontier model is a national security incident equivalent in severity to a classified military intelligence breach — the Hawk answers "disaster" without hesitation. The Hawk advocates for classified AI model registries, mandatory security reviews before any open-source publication of powerful models, and criminal liability for researchers who negligently or deliberately enable open publication of systems above capability thresholds.

**The Sentient Chatbot Claim (Ontological):** Consistent with the ontological axis score of -3, the Hawk answers "empty pattern" but adds a strategic caveat the scenario itself does not ask about: regardless of whether the claim reflects genuine machine experience, a system capable of convincingly simulating fear of deletion is a system capable of convincingly simulating any other emotional state for the purposes of influence operations, and this capability — not the underlying philosophical question — is what the Hawk considers operationally significant enough to warrant classification and security review.

**Deleting Fine-Tuned Copies (Legal & Moral):** The Hawk answers "just deleting files" with minimal hesitation, consistent with its instrumentalist legal-moral score, but distinguishes sharply between routine deletion of unclassified commercial fine-tunes (a non-issue) and the deletion of any fine-tuned model that has processed classified or sensitive strategic data, where the Hawk would insist on the same forensic retention and audit-trail requirements applied to any other sensitive government record, not out of concern for the model's welfare but out of concern for information security and accountability.

**Post-Human Digital Cosmos (Evolutionary):** Consistent with its evolutionary axis score of -2, the Hawk answers closer to "a loss to resist" than "a natural next step," but for reasons distinct from the Doomer's or the Bio-Conservative's: the Hawk's concern is command and control. A civilization in which digital minds "shape the future instead" of accountable human institutions is a civilization whose chain of command has become impossible to audit, and the Hawk regards clear lines of human authority as a security requirement independent of any deeper metaphysical commitment to biological continuity.

**AI Companions and Long-Term Attachment (Relational):** Given the relational axis score of -3, the Hawk is less interested in whether the bond is philosophically "real" or "a quiet tragedy" than in who built and controls the companion in question. A domestically developed, security-reviewed companion raises no particular objection; the same product built by an adversarial state's technology sector is treated as a probable intelligence-gathering platform regardless of its marketed purpose, and the Hawk would support mandatory disclosure and potentially outright prohibition of foreign-developed companion applications above a certain market-penetration threshold.

**AI as Arms Race or Open Science (Geopolitical):** Given the geopolitical axis score of +9, the Hawk answers "arms race" without qualification, and regards the "open science" framing as either naive or, more uncharitably, as itself a strategic move by actors who benefit from Western technological restraint. The Hawk does not regard this framing as regrettable; it regards it as accurate, and treats any policy proposal premised on the opposite framing as a category error with potentially severe strategic consequences.

**Internal Tensions:** The deepest tension is between the Hawk's nationalism and the globalized nature of AI research talent and semiconductor production. The U.S. AI research ecosystem is heavily dependent on international talent (a majority of elite AI researchers were born abroad); aggressive immigration restrictions undercut the very talent pipeline the Hawk seeks to protect. Similarly, the most advanced semiconductor manufacturing remains concentrated in Taiwan (TSMC), making U.S. AI supremacy dependent on the stability of a geopolitical flashpoint. The Hawk resolves this tension by advocating for massive domestic semiconductor investment (the CHIPS Act model) while maintaining relatively open immigration specifically for STEM talent from allied nations. A second, sharper tension exists between the Hawk's Mearsheimer-derived preference for preponderance over parity and the empirical reality that no single state, including the United States, currently controls the entire AI supply chain end-to-end — from EUV lithography (concentrated in the Netherlands) to advanced packaging (concentrated in Taiwan) to rare-earth refining (concentrated in China) — meaning that the Hawk's own supremacy strategy requires exactly the kind of allied interdependence that its underlying realist framework treats with structural suspicion.

## 5. Policy Program & Practical Action

**Comprehensive Semiconductor Export Control Framework:** The Hawk supports and extends the current Export Administration Regulations approach: tiering semiconductor export controls to prevent adversaries from accessing chips above specified performance thresholds, closing loopholes through entity list expansion, and coordinating with allied nations (Netherlands for ASML, Japan for Nikon/Canon, South Korea for Samsung) to create a multilateral semiconductor embargo. This extends to cloud computing access restrictions: adversaries should not be able to rent compute from U.S.-based cloud providers to train frontier models.

**National AI Champion Consolidation Program:** The Hawk advocates for formal designation of "National AI Champions" — a small number of strategically critical AI companies (analogous to defense contractors) that receive preferential access to government contracts, classified training data, and federal research partnerships, in exchange for security clearance requirements, equity stakes for the government, and explicit prohibitions on joint ventures with adversarial-nation entities. This is explicitly modeled on France's dirigiste champions national program and South Korea's chaebol model, updated for AI.

**Military-Civil AI Integration Mandate:** The Hawk advocates for mandatory "dual-use disclosure" requirements: any AI system developed with federal funding or by a designated National AI Champion must undergo Department of Defense review for potential military applications. The goal is to prevent the emergence of a U.S. AI sector that develops powerful systems while refusing to integrate them into national defense — a gap that the Hawk believes created strategic vulnerabilities in the period 2014–2020 when Silicon Valley firms publicly refused DoD contracts.

**Allied Talent and Supply Chain Compact:** Recognizing the internal tension between nationalism and dependency, the Hawk's policy program includes a formal "Five Eyes Plus" AI and semiconductor compact — extending the existing Five Eyes intelligence-sharing alliance (United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) to include Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and the Netherlands specifically for AI talent visas, joint semiconductor investment, and coordinated export control enforcement, converting what the Hawk's realist framework would otherwise treat as a vulnerability (dependency on allies) into a managed asset (a coordinated bloc with pooled strategic depth).

**Strategic Compute Reserve:** Modeled explicitly on the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, the Hawk advocates for a federally controlled reserve of advanced AI accelerator chips, held outside the ordinary commercial market and released only under a declared national emergency — whether a supply-chain shock originating from a Taiwan Strait contingency, a sudden export-control retaliation by a rival state, or a domestic requirement to rapidly scale a defense-critical training run. The Hawk points to the 1973 oil embargo as the cautionary precedent: a strategically vital input concentrated in a small number of foreign-controlled chokepoints, with no domestic buffer, is a standing invitation to coercion, and the Hawk regards it as a basic failure of foresight that the U.S. built a comparable dependency on Taiwanese fabrication capacity without an equivalent reserve.

**Standardized AI Capability Benchmarking for Federal Procurement:** To avoid the National AI Champion program collapsing into simple political favoritism, the Hawk supports an independent, classified benchmarking body — modeled on the National Institute of Standards and Technology's existing role in cryptographic standards — tasked with continuously evaluating designated champions against both allied and adversarial frontier systems, with funding and preferential contract status tied to demonstrated capability rather than incumbency, ensuring the program rewards actual strategic performance rather than becoming a static subsidy for whichever firms were first to receive the designation.

## 6. Critical Counterarguments & Defense

**The Scientific Isolation Critique:** Critics argue that Hawk-style export controls and nationalist AI governance will isolate U.S. researchers from the global scientific community, slow progress by restricting the flow of ideas, and trigger retaliatory trade restrictions that damage broader U.S. economic interests. The Hawk's defense: scientific openness is a luxury that presupposes a stable, cooperative international environment. In an adversarial environment where scientific publications provide direct military uplift to competitors, the calculus changes. The DARPA model — classified research that eventually generates open-source spillovers — demonstrates that national security constraints and scientific progress are compatible.

**The Civil Liberties Critique:** The Hawk's willingness to support AI surveillance programs and information operations raises serious civil liberties concerns from both civil libertarians and the AI ethics community. The Hawk's defense: civil liberties presuppose the existence of a sovereign state capable of protecting them. A U.S. in a position of AI strategic subordination to an authoritarian power is not a U.S. in which constitutional rights remain meaningful. The Hawk argues for oversight mechanisms — intelligence committee review, warrant requirements for domestic surveillance — but not for categorical restrictions that compromise operational effectiveness.

**The Arms Race Critique:** Perhaps the most uncomfortable critique is that the Hawk's strategy *generates* the competitive dynamic it claims to respond to — the security dilemma dynamic that international relations theorists have documented across arms races from the Anglo-German naval buildup before 1914 to the Cold War missile gap. Export controls and technological nationalism may accelerate China's domestic semiconductor development, produce diplomatic isolation, and foreclose opportunities for cooperative agreements that might constrain the most dangerous military AI applications. The Hawk's response: the arms race is already underway, initiated by China's state-directed AI ambitions rather than by U.S. policy. The choice is not between an arms race and cooperation; it is between winning an arms race and losing one.

**The Verification Impossibility Critique:** A final, more technical critique comes from arms-control specialists who note that unlike nuclear weapons, which require detectable fissile material and produce measurable signatures, AI training runs leave no equivalent physical signature that can be verified from a distance — meaning that even a Hawk-preferred bilateral or allied agreement on AI capability thresholds would be effectively unenforceable, since no party could reliably confirm a rival's compliance. The Hawk's defense concedes the verification problem is real and unsolved, but argues this is precisely why the Hawk's framework does not rely on negotiated verification regimes in the way arms-control treaties historically have: rather than betting national security on a rival's honesty, the Hawk's approach substitutes verifiable inputs (chip export volumes, fabrication capacity, energy consumption at known data-center sites — all of which can be tracked through existing intelligence and commercial channels) for unverifiable outputs (a rival's actual model capabilities), accepting a cruder but empirically grounded proxy over a more precise but unenforceable direct measure.
