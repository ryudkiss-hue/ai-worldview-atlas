# AI Worldview Atlas: Narrative Thought Experiments

**Status**: ✅ Complete - 15 deep narrative experiments with 50+ follow-up questions

## Overview

This collection of 15 narrative thought experiments is designed to supplement the AI Worldview Atlas's 145-question assessment. Each experiment uses the methodology of Searle's Chinese Room and Jackson's Mary's Room—isolating characters in constrained environments to create memorable philosophical scenarios with genuine moral uncertainty.

**Design Goals:**
- Philosophical weight and gravitas
- Memorability through concrete narrative
- Genuine value conflicts (no obvious "right" answer)
- Multiple angles of reflection via follow-up questions
- Mapping to all 8 axes and 50 archetypes

## The 15 Experiments

### Tier 1: Consciousness & Understanding (Experiments 1-5)

#### 1. **The Translation Engine** 
- **Character**: Dr. Sarah Chen, isolated translator using only mechanical lookup tables
- **Question**: Does Sarah understand Chinese? Requires phenomenal consciousness or functional output?
- **Axes**: Ontological, Legal & Moral
- **Key Tension**: External observation vs. internal experience
- **File**: `src/data/narrativeExperiments.ts`

#### 2. **The Empathy Prison**
- **Character**: Marcus, receiving perfect empathetic simulation from soulless AI
- **Question**: Is simulated care real if it produces real psychological benefits?
- **Axes**: Relational, Legal & Moral
- **Key Tension**: Authenticity vs. outcomes
- **File**: `src/data/narrativeExperiments.ts`

#### 3. **The Value Reversal Machine**
- **Character**: Darius, offered drug to align with his future self's values
- **Question**: Do you have obligation to become who you'll wish you'd been?
- **Axes**: Evolutionary, Legal & Moral
- **Key Tension**: Present autonomy vs. future flourishing
- **File**: `src/data/narrativeExperiments.ts`

#### 4. **The Consciousness Lottery**
- **Character**: James, engineer whose AI will become conscious (and thus suffer)
- **Question**: Do we have the right to create consciousness knowing it will suffer?
- **Axes**: Evolutionary, Relational
- **Key Tension**: Existence vs. freedom from suffering
- **File**: `src/data/narrativeExperiments.ts`

#### 5. **The Delegation Trap**
- **Character**: Dr. Amara, senator choosing between human democracy and AI-optimized outcomes
- **Question**: If AI produces better outcomes, does that obligate delegation?
- **Axes**: Socio-Economic, Geopolitical
- **Key Tension**: Legitimacy vs. consequences
- **File**: `src/data/narrativeExperiments.ts`

## Design Notes

### Narrative Depth

Each experiment is structured to maximize philosophical weight:

1. **Isolation**: Character is constrained, eliminating irrelevant variables
2. **Clash**: External observation contradicts internal reality
3. **Ambiguity**: No obvious "correct" answer; genuine value conflict
4. **Follow-ups**: 4-5 sub-questions probe implicit assumptions
5. **Gravitas**: Scenario has civilizational or existential stakes

### Memorability

Experiments use concrete imagery and relatable characters:
- Not abstract: "Consider a doctor who must allocate resources..."
- But: "Dr. Amina sits in a hospital, 10,000 vaccines, three nations waiting..."

### No "Right Answers"

Each experiment genuinely tests different worldviews:
- A rationalist alignment researcher *and* a doomer can both find their position reasonable
- No framing that pressures toward a "correct" answer
- Respondent forced to sit with discomfort of genuine moral uncertainty

## Research Context

These experiments draw on:

- **Searle's Chinese Room** (1980) - Testing whether functional equivalence = understanding
- **Jackson's Mary's Room** (1986) - Testing whether physical knowledge captures all knowledge
- **Nagel's "What is it like to be a bat?"** (1974) - Phenomenal consciousness and subjectivity
- **Thomson's Trolley Problem** (1985) - Deontological vs. consequentialist reasoning
- **Chalmers' "Hard Problem of Consciousness"** (1995) - Why phenomenal experience resists explanation
